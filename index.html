The Rich History of ASL

American Sign Language (ASL) is not just English on the handsâ€”it is a complete, natural language with its own complex grammar. Its journey began in 1817 when Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet and Laurent Clerc founded the first school for the deaf in Hartford, Connecticut.

ASL was born from a mix of French Sign Language (LSF) and local American "village signs." For years, the language faced "Oralism," where signing was banned in schools to force speech. However, the Deaf community fought for their linguistic rights, culminating in the "Deaf President Now" movement of 1988, which solidified ASL's place as a sophisticated and respected language in academia and culture.

Key Beginner Tips

  • Eye Contact: In ASL, eye contact is as important as the signs themselves. Looking away is like walking away during a conversation.
  • Facial Grammar: Your eyebrows and mouth act as the "tone" of your voice. A raised eyebrow can change a statement into a question.
  • Dominant Hand: Always use your primary hand (the one you write with) to lead your signs to avoid confusing the viewer.
