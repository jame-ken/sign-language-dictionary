The ASL Alphabet

Mastering fingerspelling is the bridge between signs and specific English terms.

A

The Fist Shape

Make a tight fist with your thumb resting vertically against the side of your index finger. Ensure your palm faces forward.

Usage Example: Use "A" to spell the name "Anna" or to clarify you mean "Atlanta" rather than another city starting with the same sign.

B

The Flat Hand

Keep your fingers together and straight up. Fold your thumb across your palm. This is often called the "Closed 5" handshape.

Usage Example: Essential for spelling titles like "The Bible" or brands like "Burger King" which may not have a dedicated regional sign.

C

The Curve

Curve your fingers and thumb to literally form the shape of the letter 'C'. Your palm should slightly face the side.

Usage Example: Used when spelling names like "Chris" or countries like "Canada." It is also the base handshape for many signs like "Class."

D

The Pointer

Point your index finger straight up. Your thumb and other three fingers should touch to form a circle at the base.

Usage Example: Use this to spell out "Denver" or "Dr. Pepper" when specifying a particular drink at a restaurant.

E

The Claw

Curl all four fingers inward so the tips are resting on top of your thumb. It looks like a small, tight claw.

Usage Example: Used in names like "Eric" or common English loanwords like "E-M-A-I-L" which is often fingerspelled quickly.

F

The OK Sign

Touch your index finger and thumb together in a circle. Keep your other three fingers spread apart and pointing up.

Usage Example: Used for spelling "Florida" or titles like "Ford" when discussing specific car manufacturers.

G

The Pinch

Point your index finger and thumb parallel to each other, pointing toward the left (for right-handed signers). It looks like you're measuring a small gap.

Usage Example: Essential for spelling names like "George" or clarifying specific terms like "Gravity" in a science lesson.

H

The Parallel Fingers

Similar to "G," but extend both your index and middle fingers together, pointing horizontally.

Usage Example: Used for "Hawaii" or spelling names like "Henry." It is a very common handshape in motion-based signs as well.

I

The Pinky

Make a fist but extend only your pinky finger straight up. Keep your palm facing forward.

Usage Example: Used for "Italy" or short names like "Ian." It is also used to sign the concept of "I" (myself) in some contexts.

J

The Hook

Start with the "I" handshape and use your pinky to draw the letter 'J' in the air, ending with your palm facing you.

Usage Example: Used for names like "James" or "June." Because it involves movement, it is one of the more unique letters to master.

K

The V-Thumb

Point your index finger up and middle finger out in a "V" shape. Place your thumb on the middle joint of your middle finger.

Usage Example: Used for "Kansas" or spelling "K-O-A-L-A." This handshape is also used for the sign "Keep."

L

The Angle

Extend your thumb and index finger to form a perfect right angle, mimicking the letter 'L'.

Usage Example: Used for "London" or "Lexus." It's a very clear, easy-to-read sign.

M

The Three-Finger Tuck

Tuck your thumb under your index, middle, and ring fingers. Your fingers should drape over the thumb.

Usage Example: Used for "Mexico" or names like "Mary." It is often confused with 'N', so keep the three fingers clear.

N

The Two-Finger Tuck

Similar to 'M', but tuck your thumb under only your index and middle fingers.

Usage Example: Used for "Norway" or "Netflix." In fast fingerspelling, the thumb placement is key to distinguish it from 'M'.

O

The Circle

Curve all fingers and thumb so they meet at the tips, forming a circle or the letter 'O'.

Usage Example: Used for "Ohio" or names like "Oscar." It is a foundational shape for signs like "None."

P

The Downward K

Make the "K" handshape but point your index finger horizontally and your middle finger straight down.

Usage Example: Used for "Paris" or "Pepsi." This is one of the letters where the "orientation" (pointing down) is the most important part.

Q

The Downward G

Make the "G" handshape but point both your index finger and thumb straight down toward the ground.

Usage Example: Used for spelling "Quebec" or names like "Quincy."

R

The Crossed Fingers

Cross your middle finger over your index finger, like you are wishing for good luck.

Usage Example: Used for "Rome" or names like "Ryan." It's also the sign for "Ready" when shaken slightly.

S

The Full Fist

Make a fist and place your thumb directly across the front of your fingers. Do not put it on the side (that would be 'A').

Usage Example: Used for "Seattle" or "Samsung." This is also the handshape for the sign "Work."

T

The Single Tuck

Make a fist and tuck your thumb between your index and middle fingers. Only the tip of the thumb should show.

Usage Example: Used for "Texas" or "Toyota."

U

The Together Pair

Extend your index and middle fingers straight up and keep them pressed tightly together.

Usage Example: Used for "Utah" or "Uber." If you spread the fingers, it becomes a 'V'.

V

The Spread Pair

Extend your index and middle fingers straight up in a "V" shape, like a peace sign.

Usage Example: Used for "Vermont" or names like "Victor."

W

The Three Fingers

Extend your index, middle, and ring fingers up and spread them apart. Your thumb and pinky should touch.

Usage Example: Used for "Washington" or "Walmart." This is also the sign for "Water" when tapped on the chin.

X

The Hook

Make a fist but extend your index finger and bend it into a hook or a "C" shape.

Usage Example: Used for "X-Ray" or names like "Xavier."

Y

The Horns

Extend your thumb and pinky finger out. Keep your three middle fingers folded into your palm.

Usage Example: Used for "Yellowstone" or names like "Yolanda." This is also the sign for "Same."

Z

The Z-Trace

Use your index finger to "draw" the letter 'Z' in the air in front of you.

Usage Example: Used for "Zimbabwe" or "Zion." Like 'J', this letter is defined by its movement.
